Sustainable Open Linguistic Research

In this course, we explore open data and open science for language knowledge through the lens of sustainability—reducing duplication, reusing existing resources, and recycling legacy data for new audiences and uses. We’ll connect practice to principles such as FAIR and reproducibility, and deliver a team project with public artifacts (data, code, documentation).

Course Aim & Learning Outcomes

  • Explain the “Reduce–Reuse–Recycle” framing for sustainable linguistic research and relate it to FAIR and reproducibility.
  • Plan and execute an open linguistic data workflow (license selection, documentation, versioning, archiving).
  • Critically evaluate existing datasets and tools for reusability and maintenance costs over time.
  • Produce a small, well-documented open dataset or enhancement to an existing one (e.g., OMW, ELAR materials, legacy dictionaries linked to WordNet) with a sustainability checklist.

Related ESCO skill example: Green Computing

The course fosters green computing competences by teaching students how to manage linguistic data in environmentally and socially sustainable ways. Through the principles of reduce, reuse, and recycle, students learn to avoid redundant data collection, repurpose existing digital resources, and maintain open datasets for long-term reusability. This approach reduces computational waste, promotes data efficiency, and aligns linguistic research with broader sustainability goals in digital infrastructure.

By applying green computing principles, students gain practical experience in sustainable digital research workflows. They learn to design and document linguistic resources that are energy-efficient, interoperable, and FAIR-compliant, ensuring long-term value with minimal environmental impact. In professional practice, these skills prepare graduates to contribute to open-science projects, digital archives, and data-driven organisations seeking to reduce their ecological footprint while promoting knowledge accessibility and reusability.

Major Assessments

  • Participation & Weekly Prep (reading notes, in-class activities): 20%
  • Open Data Critique (Reduce/Reuse/Recycle case study on an existing resource; 1.5–3 pp + checklist): 15%
  • Team Project: sustainable open linguistic resource
    • Project proposal (Week 7–9): 10%
    • Implementation & documentation (Weeks 10–11): 30%
    • Public artifact (data + code + README + LICENSE + CITATION.cff + archive DOI): 15%
    • Final presentation (Week 12): 10%

Submission & Openness

  • All deliverables must include: clear license (preferably CC BY / CC0 / OSI-approved), provenance, versioning, and a sustainability/maintenance note.
  • Archive final data/code on Zenodo (DOI) or equivalent; mirror on GitHub with a release.
  • Where appropriate, contribute to existing platforms (e.g., OMW, Wikipedia, ELAR cross-links).

Policies & Practicalities

  • Attendance: Regular attendance and active participation are required.
  • Academic integrity: Cite all sources; include data/code licenses; document provenance.
  • Accessibility: Provide alt-text for figures, captions for audio/video where feasible.
  • Tools: GitHub, Zenodo, Wikidata/Wikipedia, OMW, ELAR (for discovery); Python/Flask/Jupyter; R as needed.
